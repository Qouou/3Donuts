Overview

The methods of 3D scanning using cameras and sculpting 3D models using 2D photos have been developed in the engineering field since the late '90s. Despite the techniques that have already been around for decades and improved throughout the years, it was not until today that the complements of the 3D model’s implementation, such as 3D printing, motion capture, and facial recognition, have grown mature enough for consumer markets. Thus, we think now is the right moment for us to kick in the field. By focusing on automating and perfecting the sculpture of 3D models and the various fields it could be implemented in, such as digital preservation of cultural heritage, clothing/accessories preview, real-time animation, and 3D printing, we wish to devote energy to using the full potential of 3D modeling. The result will then be presented as a refined website endorsed by a strong business model, hopefully.

How we use a large number of photo to create 3D models

3D Scanning Apparatus

As might be expected, we can’t start our implementations without having a working method to obtain 3D models. Sure the model could be sculpted from scratch by using software such as Blender, SketchUp, or Maya. But there's another emphasis of our project, which is turning real-world objects into 3D models. Our approach is to build the model by using sets of cameras, which the photos taken will then be transformed into 3D models.

  • Camera Model: Canon EOS 200D
  • Quantity: 120
  • Fund source: Ministry of Science and Technology

We have a larger studio for use of medium-to-large-sized objects, such as figure photography, sculptures, or pets. And a smaller one for detail-needed small objects which require careful alignment, environment, and aperture setup. The large studio was built with 12 sets of multi-camera groups arranged in a circle, each 8 with 10 DSLRs (Canon EOS 200D) and 3 studio LED panels from top to bottom. Using CaptureGRID, our master will then be able to take control of the total 120 cameras by communicating with the 12 hosts, that each host has 10 cameras. As for the smaller one, the apparatus was built by our supervisor, Dr. Chen. It has mainly two parts, a metal arm where 4 cameras were attached to, combining with a turntable which the subject sits on. With a Python program named “JC.py”, which is also written by our supervisor, the turntable was set to rotate for a specific angle multiple times, and every time a rotation happens the cameras take a set of pictures. After a full circle is done, a 360 view of the object will then be captured. With a combination of Rclone and shell script, a function of auto-uploading the photos taken to our Google Drive account was also added. For which makes our workflow more seamlessly since we now gain access to the photo sets from the much powerful PC effortlessly. The beefy PC will then take care of the forming of our 3D model using Metashape (current solution) or RealityCapture (alternative candidate).
